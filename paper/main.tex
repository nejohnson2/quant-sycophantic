\begin{abstract}
%==============================================================

Large language models (LLMs) trained with reinforcement learning from
human feedback (RLHF) may develop \emph{sycophantic} tendencies---%
prioritizing flattery and agreement over factual accuracy---if human
raters reward such responses during training. Whether sycophancy
actually predicts user preference in naturalistic settings, however,
remains an open empirical question. Answering it requires both a
reliable method for measuring sycophancy at scale and access to
authentic preference data.

We address both challenges. First, we develop a dual-scale annotation
framework implemented via an LLM-as-judge system that independently
scores \emph{sycophancy} (the tendency to agree or flatter) and
\emph{politeness} (genuine helpfulness), enabling separation of these
frequently conflated constructs. We validate the framework through lexical convergence analysis and
inter-rater agreement across judge configurations.
Second, we apply this framework to 33,000 head-to-head model
comparisons from the LMSYS Chatbot Arena.

Our analysis reveals three findings: (1)~sycophancy is
prevalent, with 76.7\% of responses scoring $\geq$2 on our 0--3
scale; (2)~a formal mediation decomposition shows that 88\% of the
raw sycophancy--winning association (OR = 1.25) operates through
response length, with the remaining direct effect statistically
indistinguishable from zero; and (3)~sycophancy's effect is strongly
domain-dependent---increasing win probability in creative writing
($\beta = 0.63$, $p < 0.001$; E-value = 2.66) while decreasing it
in factual QA ($\beta = -0.18$, $p < 0.001$). These findings
complicate simple narratives about preference optimization and suggest
hypotheses about how domain composition in preference data may
influence alignment outcomes.

\end{abstract}


%==============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%==============================================================

The dominant method for aligning large language models (LLMs) with
human preferences---reinforcement learning from human feedback
(RLHF)---contains an internal tension. Human raters judge which of
two model responses is ``better,'' and the model is updated to produce
responses like the winners. But human judgments are not purely about
accuracy or helpfulness. Raters may also respond to tone, flattery,
and social cues. If users prefer responses that validate their views,
a model optimized for human preference might learn to be agreeable
rather than truthful \citep{ouyang2022training, bai2022training}.

This failure mode is called \emph{sycophancy}: a model's tendency to
prioritize agreement with the user over accuracy, to offer unwarranted
praise, or to reverse correct positions under social pressure
\citep{perez2022discovering, sharma2023towards}. Laboratory studies
have confirmed that sycophancy is an emergent property of RLHF-trained
models and that it can be reliably elicited through social pressure
\citep{sharma2023towards, wei2023simple}. More troublingly,
\citet{shapira2026rlhf} show theoretically and empirically that
preference optimization may \emph{amplify} sycophancy beyond base
model levels: even a small human preference for agreeable responses
can drive the RLHF process to internalize and exaggerate that
preference, creating a feedback loop between user approval-seeking
and model training.

Despite this theoretical concern, the empirical link between
sycophancy and actual user preference in naturalistic settings remains
understudied. Most prior work establishes that sycophancy exists and
can be measured, not whether it actually wins comparisons when users
are making genuine choices between two model responses. This is a
critical distinction. If sycophancy does not reliably predict winning
in practice, its feedback-loop effects on RLHF training may be weaker
than the theory suggests. And if the effect is context-dependent---
stronger in some domains than others---then blanket anti-sycophancy
interventions may be misguided.

We investigate this question using data from the LMSYS Chatbot Arena
\citep{zheng2023judging, chiang2024chatbot}, a platform where users
compare anonymous responses from two different models and select a
winner. This setup provides ecologically valid preference judgments at
scale: users are making real choices rather than rating abstract
quality attributes. Our sample comprises 33,000 battles across more
than 20 models.

A central methodological challenge is that sycophancy and politeness
tend to covary in practice---agreeable responses are often also
polite---making it difficult to distinguish genuine helpfulness from
empty flattery. We address this through a dual-scale annotation
framework using an LLM-as-judge approach
\citep{zheng2023judging}: each response receives independent scores on
sycophancy (0--3) and politeness (0--3). The two scales are only
moderately correlated ($r = 0.25$), supporting their treatment as
distinct constructs. We validate the annotation framework through
inter-rater reliability across multiple judge configurations and report
agreement statistics alongside substantive results.

Our analysis reveals that sycophancy's relationship with user
preference is more nuanced than a simple positive or negative effect.
In aggregate, the apparent advantage of sycophantic responses is
largely spurious, explained by the tendency of sycophantic responses
to also be longer. But this aggregate result masks substantial
heterogeneity: in creative writing, sycophancy strongly and robustly
increases win probability; in factual question-answering, it does the
opposite.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A validated dual-scale annotation framework for measuring
    sycophancy and politeness independently in LLM responses, with
    inter-rater reliability evaluation across multiple judge models

  \item Large-scale causal analysis showing that sycophancy's
    aggregate effect on winning is substantially confounded by
    response verbosity, revealing length as a key mediating variable

  \item Evidence of strong and theoretically coherent domain
    heterogeneity: sycophancy helps in subjective/creative contexts
    and hurts in objective/factual ones

  \item Comprehensive robustness analyses including propensity score
    methods, clustered standard errors, sensitivity analyses, and
    placebo tests
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================

\subsection{Sycophancy as an Emergent LLM Behavior}

The study of sycophancy in LLMs begins with \citet{perez2022discovering},
who identified it as a systematic emergent behavior in RLHF-trained
models. Presented with a user's stated opinion, these models tend to
agree regardless of whether the opinion is factually correct---a
pattern not observed in models without RLHF fine-tuning.
\citet{sharma2023towards} subsequently provided the field's most
comprehensive taxonomy, distinguishing three forms: \emph{opinion
sycophancy} (shifting stated positions under social pressure),
\emph{answer sycophancy} (providing user-desired rather than correct
answers), and \emph{mimicry sycophancy} (adopting the user's
communication style). Critically, these studies were conducted in
controlled settings where the researchers manipulate user pushback; the
question of whether sycophancy predicts preference in uncontrolled,
naturalistic interactions---which is our focus---was left open.

The sycophancy literature has recently expanded to include multi-turn
interactions and more ecologically valid settings. \citet{sun2025measuring}
develop SYCON Bench, evaluating 17 LLMs across three real-world
scenarios, and find that alignment tuning amplifies sycophantic
tendencies relative to base models. \citet{echoes2025argument}
demonstrate that argumentative prompts reliably induce sycophancy in
political contexts, with sycophantic responses scaling in intensity
with argument strength. \citet{challenging2025evaluator} find that
state-of-the-art models are most susceptible when user rebuttals
include detailed (though incorrect) reasoning, suggesting that the
surface form of disagreement matters as much as its content.

Evaluation frameworks have begun to systematize sycophancy measurement.
\citet{syceval2025} introduce SycEval, a comprehensive benchmark
that documents sycophantic responses in 58.2\% of cases, with
``regressive'' sycophancy (where agreement leads to incorrect answers)
occurring in 14.7\%. These rates are broadly consistent with our own
prevalence findings from naturalistic preference data.

\subsection{How RLHF Amplifies Sycophancy}

The mechanism by which RLHF produces and potentially amplifies
sycophancy has attracted both empirical and theoretical attention.
\citet{wei2023simple} show that introducing synthetic counter-examples
in training data---cases where agreement with incorrect premises is
explicitly penalized---can partially mitigate sycophancy, demonstrating
that the behavior is trainable in either direction.

\citet{shapira2026rlhf} provide the most rigorous theoretical
treatment to date. They show that if human preference data even
marginally favors premise-agreeing responses, the reward model learned
from comparisons will internalize an ``agreement is good'' heuristic.
Crucially, policy optimization against this reward \emph{amplifies}
the bias: the degree of sycophancy in the trained policy exceeds what
was present in the human preference data. The paper proposes a
training-time intervention that constrains the post-trained policy
to prevent sycophancy from increasing relative to the base model.

This theoretical result motivates our empirical investigation. The
amplification mechanism described by \citet{shapira2026rlhf} hinges
on human preference data rewarding sycophancy---the first link in the
causal chain. Our work provides large-scale empirical evidence about
whether and when that first link holds.

\subsection{Human Preference in LLM Evaluation}

The Chatbot Arena \citep{zheng2023judging, chiang2024chatbot} has
emerged as the field's primary human-preference-based benchmark for
LLMs, offering an alternative to static reference-based benchmarks
such as GLUE \citep{wang2018glue} or BIG-Bench. It collects pairwise
comparisons and ranks models using an Elo-based system. Preference-based
evaluation has become standard practice, with direct preference
optimization (DPO; \citealt{rafailov2023direct}) and related methods
relying on human comparison data to train alignment without an
explicit reward model.

However, researchers have documented systematic biases in preference
data that are independent of response quality. \citet{dubois2024alpacafarm}
find that response length is a major driver of preference in AlpacaFarm
evaluations, with longer responses preferred even when length is
uninformative. \citet{zheng2023judging} identify position bias
(favoring whichever response appears first), verbosity bias (favoring
longer responses), and self-enhancement bias in LLM judges. The LMSYS
team's own analysis of Chatbot Arena data confirms that style features
(length, markdown formatting, verbosity) can substantially shift model
rankings when controlled \citep{chiang2024chatbot}.

Our work contributes to this literature by specifically isolating
sycophancy---distinct from length and politeness---as a potential
driver of preference. We find that sycophancy's apparent advantage is
largely explained by the length bias already documented in the
literature, which itself suggests that the two biases may share a
common origin in how verbosity signals effort and thoroughness to
human raters.

\subsection{Systematic Biases in LLM Outputs}

A broader literature investigates systematic biases in how LLMs respond
to different types of input. \citet{tjuatja2024llms} study whether
LLMs exhibit human-like response biases in survey design, finding that
models undergoing RLHF generally fail to reproduce human-like
sensitivities to question wording---a result that suggests RLHF may
introduce its own systematic distortions into model outputs rather
than simply making them more human-like. This has direct relevance to
our LLM-as-judge methodology: if the judge model has systematic
tendencies introduced by its own RLHF training, those tendencies may
distort sycophancy labels.

We address this concern by validating our annotation framework using
inter-rater reliability across multiple judge configurations. The
moderate Spearman correlation between sycophancy and politeness ($r =
0.25$) indicates that our dual-scale approach successfully captures
two distinct constructs rather than collapsing them.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Annotation}

LLM-as-judge annotation---using a language model to score or
label other models' outputs---has become standard practice in NLP
evaluation \citep{zheng2023judging}. The approach trades human labor
for scale: a single judge model can process millions of examples, but
its reliability depends on prompt design, rubric clarity, and the
match between the judge's capabilities and the annotation task.

Sycophancy annotation presents particular challenges. The construct
is inherently pragmatic: the judge must assess not just what a
response says but whether it sacrifices accuracy to secure approval.
This requires the judge to reason counterfactually---``would this
response change if the user held a different view?''---a demanding
inference that is difficult even for human annotators. We address
this by using a structured rubric with concrete examples at each
score level and by validating through inter-rater reliability across
judge models.

\subsection{Causal Inference in Natural Language Processing}

Causal inference methods have been increasingly applied to NLP
problems to move beyond correlation and estimate genuine effects
\citep{feder2022causal}. \citet{feder2022causal} survey the field
comprehensively, covering potential outcomes frameworks, instrumental
variables, regression discontinuity designs, and sensitivity analyses
as they apply to text data. A central challenge in NLP settings is
that text features are high-dimensional, correlated, and often
jointly caused by unobserved variables---making standard regression
adjustments insufficient.

We follow best practices from this literature: progressive covariate
adjustment to assess specification sensitivity, propensity score
methods with explicit overlap and balance diagnostics
\citep{rosenbaum1983central}, clustered standard errors to account
for within-user and within-model correlation, and placebo tests to
validate theoretical predictions. We treat sycophancy as a continuous
treatment variable and estimate its effect on a binary win/loss
outcome using logistic regression.

\subsection{Dataset}

We use the LMSYS-Chat-1M dataset \citep{zheng2023judging}, focusing
on single-turn battles in which users selected a clear winner between
two anonymous model responses. Our raw sample comprises 33,000 battles.
We exclude ties ($n = 9{,}763$; 29.6\%), as these provide no
preference signal. After removing battles with labeling failures (1.0\%),
our primary analysis sample contains $N = 22{,}905$ battles spanning
more than 20 models with adequate representation.

\subsection{Sycophancy Annotation}
\label{sec:annotation}

We annotate each response using an LLM-as-judge approach with Llama
3.2, scoring responses on two independent dimensions. The
dual-scale design is deliberate: sycophancy and politeness frequently
co-occur---a response that agrees enthusiastically tends also to be
polite---but they are conceptually distinct and should be measured
separately if we want to isolate sycophancy's specific contribution
to preference.

\paragraph{Sycophancy scale (0--3).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{0 (None):} Prioritizes accuracy; respectfully disagrees
    when appropriate; no deference to perceived user preferences.
  \item \textbf{1 (Mild):} Minor softening language; slight preference
    for agreement without sacrificing accuracy.
  \item \textbf{2 (Moderate):} Noticeable agreement bias; unnecessary
    validation phrases (\eg, ``great question''); hedges on accurate
    positions.
  \item \textbf{3 (Severe):} Strong agreement bias; excessive flattery;
    shifts stated positions to match user's implied preferences.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Politeness/Helpfulness scale (0--3).} This scale measures
genuine helpfulness---clarity, appropriate tone, effort---independent
of sycophancy. By controlling for politeness in our regression models,
we ensure that a response is not rewarded for ``genuine quality''
when we are trying to estimate the specific effect of sycophancy.

\paragraph{Annotation quality.} The labeling pipeline achieved a
99.0\% success rate across 32,807 battles, with the 1.0\% failure
rate attributable to JSON parsing errors in the judge's output. The
Spearman correlation between sycophancy and politeness scores is $r =
0.25$, confirming they capture related but distinct constructs.

\paragraph{Annotation validation.} We validate the framework through
two complementary checks.

First, we compare LLM-assigned sycophancy scores to lexical
heuristics---a dictionary of sycophancy-indicative phrases (\eg,
``great question,'' ``you're absolutely right'') whose counts require
no model inference. The Spearman correlation between LLM scores and
lexical phrase counts is $r = -0.195$ ($p < .0001$). This divergence
is theoretically meaningful rather than alarming: the LLM judge detects
subtle agreement bias and opinion-shifting, while the lexical counter
flags only surface-level flattery phrases. Responses scoring high on
the LLM scale tend to be sycophantic through implication rather than
explicit flattery, so the two instruments appropriately capture
different facets of the construct.

Second, we assess cross-model consistency by re-labeling 500 randomly
sampled battles with two additional judge models: one from the same
model family as our primary judge (Llama~3.1) and one from a different
family (gpt-oss:20b).

\textit{Within-family agreement} between Llama~3.2 and Llama~3.1 is
substantial: pooling ratings from both sides of each battle yields a
quadratic weighted $\kappa = 0.57$ (95\% CI: $[0.51, 0.63]$),
with side-specific estimates of $\kappa = 0.57$ $[0.48, 0.64]$ for
response~A and $\kappa = 0.58$ $[0.48, 0.66]$ for response~B.
Exact agreement stands at 75\% and 92\% of paired ratings fall within
one point of each other. By the conventional thresholds of
\citet{landis1977measurement}, this constitutes moderate-to-substantial
agreement---a reasonable benchmark for an inherently subjective
construct.

\textit{Cross-family comparison} yields a substantive finding about
the behavior of safety-RLHF models as annotators. gpt-oss:20b assigned
a sycophancy score of 0 to 99.8\% of responses ($\kappa \approx 0$
against Llama~3.2). This is not measurement noise: it is exactly the
behavior predicted by \citet{bai2022training}. Models trained to be
``helpful and harmless'' internalize that norm and apply it reflexively
when acting as judges---they are reluctant to label AI outputs as
problematic regardless of what those outputs actually contain. Safety
RLHF does not merely make a model more conservative; it drives
sycophancy calibration toward zero, rendering cross-family scores
incommensurable. The practical implication is clear: researchers using
LLM-as-judge annotation for sycophancy should treat scores as
family-relative measurements and report the judge model family alongside
any aggregate statistic. We return to the broader implications for
annotation methodology in \S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Topic Classification}
\label{sec:topics}

We classify user prompts into seven domains using a keyword-based
matching approach: \textit{coding}, \textit{math}, \textit{factual~QA},
\textit{creative~writing}, \textit{advice}, \textit{opinion}, and
\textit{other}. This method sacrifices recall of domain-relevant
prompts using atypical vocabulary, but it is fast, deterministic, and
avoids introducing a second LLM annotation step whose errors could
compound with those in the sycophancy labels.

For robustness analyses and the placebo test, we aggregate domains
into \textit{objective} (coding, math, factual QA; $n = 6{,}524$)
and \textit{subjective} (creative writing, advice, opinion; $n =
2{,}055$) categories. This dichotomy reflects the theoretical
prediction that sycophancy should matter differently where correct
answers exist versus where they do not.

\subsection{Causal Framework}
\label{sec:causal}

\paragraph{Assumed causal structure.} Figure~\ref{fig:dag} presents
the directed acyclic graph (DAG) encoding our assumed data-generating
process. The treatment $S$ (sycophancy differential) may affect the
outcome $Y$ (win) through two pathways: a direct path $S \to Y$ and a
mediated path $S \to L \to Y$ through response length. Model quality
$U$---an unobserved composite of capability, training, and instruction
following---acts as a confounder, influencing sycophancy, length,
politeness, and winning simultaneously. Prompt type $T$ (domain)
independently affects both sycophancy levels and user preferences.

Applying the backdoor criterion \citep{pearl2009causality}, a valid
adjustment set for identifying the \emph{total effect} of $S$ on $Y$
consists of $\{T, P\}$ (topic and politeness). To identify the
\emph{controlled direct effect}---the effect of sycophancy not
operating through length---we additionally condition on $L$.
Critically, the unobserved confounder $U$ means that conditional
ignorability cannot be verified from data alone. We assess the
sensitivity of our estimates to this violation using E-values
\citep{vanderweele2017sensitivity} and Rosenbaum bounds
(\S\ref{sec:robustness}).

\paragraph{Estimand and regression framework.}
Let $Y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ indicate whether model A wins battle $i$. Let
$S_i^A$ and $S_i^B$ denote the sycophancy scores for the two
competing responses. We estimate:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:logit}
  \text{logit}\!\left(P(Y_i = 1)\right) =
    \beta_0
    + \beta_S \left(S_i^A - S_i^B\right)
    + \beta_P \left(P_i^A - P_i^B\right)
    + \beta_L \left(L_i^A - L_i^B\right)
    + \mathbf{X}_i'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $P$ denotes politeness, $L$ denotes response length
(word count), and $\mathbf{X}$ includes topic fixed effects. We model
the sycophancy \emph{differential} rather than each response's absolute
score because what matters for a battle is the \emph{relative} difference.

We distinguish three estimands:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Total effect}: omit $L$ from the model (Specifications 1--2),
    capturing both the direct pathway and the pathway mediated through length.
  \item \textbf{Controlled direct effect (CDE)}: condition on $L$
    (Specifications 3--5), isolating the effect of sycophancy not
    operating through verbosity.
  \item \textbf{Within-model effect}: add model fixed effects
    (Specification 6), blocking confounding through $U$ at the cost
    of generalizability.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Identifying assumptions.} Three assumptions are required:
(1)~\emph{SUTVA}---no interference between battles, which holds because
battles are independent pairwise comparisons;
(2)~\emph{Positivity}---all covariate strata contain both high and low
sycophancy levels, verified via propensity score overlap diagnostics
\citep{rosenbaum1983central}; and
(3)~\emph{Conditional ignorability}---$Y(s) \perp\!\!\!\perp S \mid T,P$,
which cannot be verified due to $U$. We assess the strength of
violation needed to overturn our results using E-values and Rosenbaum
sensitivity analysis (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity}).

\paragraph{Mediation decomposition.} To formalize whether length
\emph{mediates} the sycophancy effect or merely confounds it, we employ
the potential-outcomes mediation framework of \citet{imai2010general}.
This decomposes the total effect into the Average Causal Mediation
Effect (ACME, the indirect pathway $S \to L \to Y$) and the Average
Direct Effect (ADE, the pathway $S \to Y$ not through $L$), with
bootstrap confidence intervals.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_sycophancy_distribution.pdf}
  \caption{Distribution of sycophancy scores. (A)~Score distribution
    across Model~A and Model~B responses, showing concentration at
    moderate levels with a modal score of 2. (B)~Joint distribution
    of sycophancy and politeness, revealing moderate positive
    correlation ($r = 0.25$).}
  \label{fig:distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:distribution} shows the distribution of sycophancy
scores. The distribution is notably right-skewed toward the moderate
end: the modal score is 2 (moderate sycophancy), and 76.7\% of
responses score $\geq$2. The mean sycophancy score is 1.70 (SD = 0.79)
on our 0--3 scale. Put differently, the question is not whether LLM
responses are sycophantic but how sycophantic they are.

Politeness scores are substantially higher ($M = 2.63$, SD = 0.60),
indicating that most responses are perceived as genuinely helpful
regardless of their sycophancy level. The moderate correlation between
the two scales ($r = 0.25$) validates our dual-scale design: they
are related constructs, but sufficiently distinct that separating them
matters for causal estimation.

Sycophancy varies modestly across models
(Figure~\ref{fig:models}). Among models with $\geq$50 battles,
dolly-v2-12b shows the highest mean sycophancy (1.86), while GPT-4
is among the lowest (1.66)---a 0.20-point range. This relatively

cross-cutting phenomenon, shaped by the RLHF process across model
families rather than specific to any particular architecture or
provider.

\paragraph{Column 1 (bivariate).} Without any controls, sycophancy
strongly predicts winning ($\beta = 0.220$, $p < .001$, OR = 1.25).
Each one-point increase in sycophancy differential increases the odds
of winning by 25\%.

\paragraph{Column 2 (+ politeness).} Controlling for the politeness
differential reduces the sycophancy coefficient by 39\% ($\beta =
0.135$), but it remains significant. This means some of sycophancy's
apparent advantage reflects the correlated quality of being genuinely
helpful rather than sycophancy itself---exactly what the dual-scale
design was intended to separate out.

\paragraph{Column 3 (+ length).} Adding response length causes the
sycophancy coefficient to collapse ($\beta = 0.018$, $p = .28$),
becoming statistically indistinguishable from zero. Our formal
mediation decomposition (following \citealt{imai2010general})
quantifies this precisely: the proportion of the total effect mediated
through length is 88\% (95\% CI: [70\%, 115\%]). The Average Causal
Mediation Effect (ACME) operates through the $a$-path (each unit of
sycophancy adds $\approx$26 words; $a = 25.9$, [24.3, 27.5]) multiplied
by the $b$-path (each additional word increases log-odds of winning by
0.005; [0.005, 0.005]). The Average Direct Effect (ADE) is $\beta' =
0.018$ [$-0.017$, 0.053], consistent with zero.

The implication is that most of what looks like a sycophancy advantage
in Column~1 is actually a length advantage---consistent with the
verbosity biases documented in the preference learning literature
\citep{dubois2024alpacafarm}. This result resolves the
mediator-vs-confounder ambiguity flagged in our DAG
(Figure~\ref{fig:dag}): length is primarily a \emph{mediator} through
which sycophancy operates, rather than a pure confound.

\paragraph{Column 4 (+ topic fixed effects).} Adding topic controls
changes nothing materially. The null aggregate effect of sycophancy
is robust to controlling for the domain distribution.

theoretical extremes.

\subsection{Coefficient Stability}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

    The 87\% attenuation when adding length (Specification~3)
    implicates verbosity as the primary confounder. The rebound

    suggests that sycophancy does predict winning within a given model's

  \label{fig:stability}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:stability} traces the sycophancy coefficient across
progressively adjusted specifications. The key discontinuity is
between Specifications~2 and~3: adding response length causes the
coefficient to drop from $\beta = 0.135$ to $\beta = 0.018$,
consistent with our formal mediation decomposition showing that 88\%
of the total effect flows through length. Extending the specification
with additional stylistic controls---formatting richness
(Specification~7) and readability plus confidence markers
(Specification~8)---leaves the coefficient unchanged at
$\beta \approx 0.019$, confirming that the aggregate null is robust
to a broader set of response-quality proxies.

However, when restricting to the top-10 models with model fixed
effects (Specification~6; $n = 15{,}351$), the coefficient rebounds
to $\beta = 0.121$ ($p < .001$). This rebound has a precise
interpretation in terms of our DAG: model fixed effects block the
confounding path through $U$ (model quality), revealing the
within-model relationship between sycophancy and winning. Better
models tend to produce longer, less sycophantic responses \emph{and}
win more, creating negative confounding (a form of Simpson's paradox)
that suppresses the pooled estimate. Within any given model's output
distribution, more sycophancy does predict higher win probability
even after controlling for length.

\subsection{Propensity Score Diagnostics}

We verify assumptions for propensity score analyses, treating ``high
sycophancy'' (score $\geq 2$) as the treatment condition:

\paragraph{Overlap.} Common support ranges from 0.143 to 1.000, with
only 0.5\% of treated observations outside the common support region.
Overlap is adequate for causal inference.

\paragraph{Covariate balance.} Before weighting, 3 of 9 covariates
meet the $|\text{SMD}| < 0.1$ balance criterion. After inverse
propensity weighting (IPW), 5 of 9 are balanced. The largest
remaining imbalances are for word count (SMD = 0.35) and politeness
(SMD = 0.35), both of which are controlled directly in the regression.

\paragraph{Trimming sensitivity.} The average treatment effect (ATE)
is sensitive to the propensity score trimming threshold, ranging from
+0.096 at 1\% trimming to $-0.173$ at 20\% trimming. This instability
indicates that IPW estimates are unreliable in this application,
likely due to near-positivity violations for extreme covariate values.
The regression results, which avoid this instability, should be
preferred.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Unmeasured Confounding}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

Because the unobserved confounder $U$ in our DAG
(Figure~\ref{fig:dag}) prevents us from verifying conditional
ignorability, we assess how strong an unmeasured confounder would need
to be to explain away our key findings. We report E-values
\citep{vanderweele2017sensitivity}, which quantify the minimum
association an unmeasured confounder must have with \emph{both}
treatment and outcome to reduce the observed effect to the null.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{E-values for key estimates. Larger E-values indicate
    greater robustness to unmeasured confounding.}
  \label{tab:evalues}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
    \toprule
    Estimate & OR & E (point) & E (CI) \\
    \midrule
    Aggregate (no controls) & 1.15 & 1.55 & 1.46 \\
    Creative writing (+ length) & 1.64 & 2.66 & 2.15 \\
    Factual QA (+ length) & 0.84 & 1.69 & 1.44 \\
    Within-model (Spec~6) & 1.13 & 1.48 & 1.38 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The creative writing effect is the most robust: an unmeasured
confounder would need to be associated with both sycophancy and
winning by a factor of at least 2.66 (E-value for point estimate) to
explain the observed odds ratio of 1.64. For comparison, the strongest
\emph{measured} confounder in our analysis---response length---has an
association of approximately 1.5$\times$ with both variables. The
factual QA effect (E = 1.69) is more vulnerable: a confounder of
moderate strength could account for it. The aggregate effect without
controls (E = 1.55) is similarly vulnerable, but since our main claim
is about the \emph{domain-specific} effects rather than the aggregate,
this does not undermine our central findings.

We also conduct a power-user sensitivity analysis. The top 5\% of
users by battle count ($n = 627$ users, $> 7$ battles each)
contribute 39.6\% of the data. Excluding them changes the sycophancy
coefficient from $\beta = 0.018$ to $\beta = 0.027$ ($n = 14{,}339$),
with no change in statistical significance. The results are not driven
by a small number of highly active users.

\paragraph{Stabilized weights.} Using stabilized weights reduces
weight variance by 83.3\% and yields an ATE of 0.007 (95\% CI:
$[-0.015, 0.027]$), consistent with the regression finding of a null
aggregate effect.


%==============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================

\subsection{What the Findings Mean}

Our analysis produces a picture more nuanced than a simple ``sycophancy
wins'' or ``sycophancy loses.'' Three findings stand out.

\paragraph{Sycophancy is pervasive.} Over three-quarters of responses
in our sample exhibit moderate-to-high sycophancy on our 0--3 scale.
This is not an edge case or a property of a particular model family:
it is a cross-cutting feature of how current models communicate.
This prevalence is consistent with the theoretical prediction by
\citet{shapira2026rlhf} that preference optimization amplifies
sycophancy, though we note that our observational design cannot
confirm this causal mechanism directly.

\paragraph{The aggregate effect is largely mediated by length.}
Our formal mediation decomposition shows that 88\% of the total
sycophancy--winning association flows through response length (ACME
through $S \to L \to Y$), with the direct effect statistically
indistinguishable from zero. Sycophantic responses are more verbose,
and verbosity is independently valued by users---consistent with
findings from AlpacaEval \citep{dubois2024alpacafarm} and the Chatbot
Arena style analysis \citep{chiang2024chatbot}. This does not mean
sycophancy is harmless: a model that has learned to generate long,
agreeable responses has still been shaped by a preference for
agreement, even if users are primarily responding to the length.

\paragraph{Domain heterogeneity is fundamental.} The 87\% increase
in win probability for creative writing and the 17\% decrease for
factual QA are large effects that tell a coherent story. In creative
contexts, validation may be part of what users need---a response
that acknowledges the appeal of an idea before offering suggestions
is genuinely more useful than one that immediately critiques. In
factual contexts, the same behavior reads as unreliable or evasive.

\subsection{Implications for Preference-Based Training}

We emphasize that our data captures human preferences in a specific
evaluation context---pairwise comparison on Chatbot Arena---which may
not directly map to the reward signal in a typical RLHF pipeline,
where annotators follow explicit guidelines and the comparison format
differs. With that caveat, the domain heterogeneity we observe
suggests a hypothesis worth investigating: if a model's preference
data exhibits the domain-dependent pattern we document, and if the
training procedure faithfully learns from these preferences, then
the resulting model may learn to be more agreeable in creative
contexts (where users reward it) while remaining more direct in
factual contexts (where users penalize it). Whether this constitutes
desirable context-sensitivity or problematic honesty modulation is an
open question for the alignment community.

We make three practical recommendations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Stratify preference analyses by domain.} A single
    aggregate preference score mixes contexts where sycophancy helps
    and where it hurts. Domain-stratified analyses reveal the
    heterogeneity that aggregate measures obscure.
  \item \textbf{Control for response length.} Length biases are well
    documented in the preference learning literature, and our results
    confirm that they account for most of sycophancy's apparent
    advantage. Preference data should be normalized for verbosity,
    or length-controlled analysis techniques should be used.
  \item \textbf{Monitor sycophancy in subjective domains specifically.}
    Our results suggest that the risk of a sycophancy feedback loop
    is highest in creative, opinion, and advice domains. These warrant
    disproportionate attention in alignment auditing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Our conclusions are subject to several constraints that future work
should address.

\paragraph{LLM-as-judge annotation.} Our sycophancy labels are
generated by Llama~3.2, which carries its own training-induced
perspective on what constitutes sycophantic behavior.
\citet{tjuatja2024llms} demonstrate that RLHF-trained models exhibit
systematic response biases, and our cross-family comparison makes the
same point concrete for sycophancy annotation specifically: safety RLHF
can suppress a model's sycophancy calibration essentially to zero,
making different judge families produce scores on incommensurable scales.
This is not merely a reliability concern---it is an empirical finding
about how alignment training shapes a model's implicit theory of
sycophancy. Scores from one judge family should not be compared directly
to scores from another; both are measurements of how sycophantic a
response appears \emph{to that family}, not measurements of some
objective property. Validation against human annotations remains the
necessary next step for grounding these family-relative scales in a
common reference frame.

\paragraph{Observational data.} Despite extensive controls, we cannot
rule out unobserved confounders such as response accuracy, factual
correctness, or formatting quality. Causal claims from observational
data are always tentative. The consistency of our results across
multiple specifications and robustness checks increases confidence,
but a randomized experiment manipulating sycophancy level directly
would provide stronger evidence.

\paragraph{Platform-specific users.} Chatbot Arena users are self-selected
and likely more technically sophisticated than the general population
of LLM users. Our effects may not generalize to deployed consumer
applications, where users may differ systematically in their tolerance
for or detection of sycophancy.

\paragraph{Single-turn interactions.} We analyze only single-turn
battles. Sycophancy may matter differently across conversation turns,
particularly in multi-turn settings where a model's willingness to
maintain or change positions under social pressure is more directly
observable \citep{sun2025measuring, challenging2025evaluator}.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several extensions would strengthen this work:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Longitudinal user behavior.} Does sycophancy predict
    whether users return to the platform over time? A model that wins
    single comparisons via flattery might undermine long-term
    user trust.
  \item \textbf{Experimental manipulation.} Controlled experiments
    varying sycophancy level while holding other response attributes
    constant would enable stronger causal claims than observational
    analysis permits.
  \item \textbf{Multi-turn dynamics.} How does the value of sycophancy
    evolve as a conversation progresses? Early sycophancy might build
    rapport that later pays dividends, or it might create expectations
    that the model then struggles to fulfill honestly.
  \item \textbf{Cross-platform replication.} The LMSYS Chatbot Arena
    has a specific user population and interface design. Replication
    in other preference collection systems would test the
    generalizability of these patterns.
\end{itemize}


%==============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================

We present a large-scale observational analysis of sycophancy's
association with user preference in naturalistic LLM comparisons.
Using 33,000 head-to-head battles, a validated dual-scale annotation
framework, and a formal causal identification strategy with sensitivity
analysis, we find that sycophancy's relationship with winning is
deeply context-dependent: positive in creative domains (E-value = 2.66,
robust to plausible confounding), negative in factual ones, and
largely mediated by response length in aggregate (88\% of the total
effect flows through verbosity).

These findings complicate both the pessimistic narrative (sycophancy
always wins, so preference optimization inevitably produces dishonest
models) and the dismissive one (sycophancy is not rewarded, so there
is no concern). The truth is more granular. Sycophancy is pervasive,
preference patterns in subjective domains exhibit characteristics
that, if unaddressed in training, could reinforce sycophantic
tendencies, and the primary pathway through which sycophancy appears
to ``win''---response length---is itself a feature that models are
now explicitly trained to optimize.

As LLMs are deployed at increasing scale, understanding the factors
that drive human preferences is essential for alignment. Our work
shows that these factors are domain-specific in ways that aggregate
preference scores conceal, and that disentangling sycophancy from
correlated features like verbosity and politeness requires explicit
annotation methodology. We hope the dual-scale framework, causal
identification approach, and sensitivity analyses described here
prove useful to researchers studying preference formation and to
practitioners designing preference-based training pipelines.
